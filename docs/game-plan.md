# Family Business Prototype Game Plan

## Vision and Core Fantasy
The player starts as a hands-on street hustler and grows into a territory-owning operator who manages people, properties, laundering, and risk. The game begins with direct street deals, personal danger, and fast money, then shifts into a broader management loop once the player owns real assets in the city.

The core fantasy is not to abandon street play, but to evolve it. In the early game, the player earns money by buying product, soliciting customers, and selling by hand while staying ahead of police pressure. In the mid and late game, the player still has the option to be active on the street, but the real growth comes from building a network of properties, dealers, stash locations, runners, and laundering fronts that work together under constant threat.

Late game should remain hybrid. The player is never reduced to a pure menu manager. They can still step into the street, move product themselves, respond to raids in person, fight gang wars, and make risky plays. The difference is that success increasingly comes from how well the network is built and defended.

## Phase-by-Phase Progression

### Early Game: Manual Hustle
The game starts with the current street-level loop. The player buys product from dealers, carries it personally, solicits customers, and closes hand-to-hand deals in active territories. Money comes in fast but is dirty, heat builds quickly, and every mistake is immediate because the player is carrying everything on them.

The player's main goals in this phase are to survive, learn the city, build territory reputation, and stack enough cash to start owning assets. The important risks are police heat, getting caught while carrying product, losing turf reputation by operating badly, and dying while overloaded with inventory.

This phase should feel personal, risky, and hands-on. The player is the whole operation.

### Mid Game Pivot: First Property
The first owned property is the hard transition point into management play. This is when the game stops being only about street deals and starts being about operating a network. Buying that first property unlocks stash management, property-based storage, dirty-versus-clean money pressure, and the first real decisions about where product and cash should live.

From this point on, the player is no longer just carrying their business with them. They are placing assets in the world, choosing where to store product, and beginning to build a structure that can outgrow direct manual selling.

The important shift here is that scale becomes possible, but exposure also rises. The player now has something to lose besides what they carry in their pockets.

### Mid Game: Network Management
Once the player owns property, the next phase is about building a working network. The player must keep building reputation in territories, claim control over areas, stock stash locations, assign dealers, and manually move product and cash between locations at first.

This part of the game should feel like an expansion phase. The player is still heavily involved on foot, but the job has changed. Instead of being only a seller, they are becoming a supplier, collector, owner, and organizer. They may still make manual sales, but the larger objective is to keep properties stocked, keep dealers earning, and begin laundering dirty money through front businesses.

The most important risks in this phase are overexpansion, raid exposure, unstable territories, gang pressure, and the difficulty of moving product and cash manually without losing it.

### End Game: Empire Sandbox
The late game is an open-ended pressure sandbox built on top of the player's network. By this point, the player can own multiple properties, control multiple territories, operate dealers out of stash-backed locations, and hire runners to automate product movement and cash collection.

The empire is stronger, but it is never safe. Large operations generate more scrutiny. Raids can hit important properties. Gang wars can destabilize contested areas. Arrests can escalate into court costs and seizures. Death creates debt and equipment loss even if the wider empire survives.

The late game goal is not simply to watch passive income tick upward. It is to balance expansion against exposure, optimize the network, defend territory, and keep the empire alive under pressure.

## Canonical System Flows

### Territory Control
Territory reputation is the first step toward control. The player must build a territory's reputation to 100 before claiming it. Reputation comes from successful activity in that area and falls when the player causes trouble, operates recklessly, or loses standing.

A territory is not fully controlled just because the player is active there. After reaching 100 reputation, the player must finish the claim in one of two ways. The first route is to pay a clean-money control fee. The second route is to win three gang wars tied to that territory. Gang wars are live territory events, not abstract menu resolutions.

A controlled territory is valuable, but it is not permanently safe. A controlled territory can fall back into contested status if the player neglects it, loses too much reputation, or suffers major setbacks such as raids or sustained instability.

### Properties and Stash Capacity
Property ownership is the backbone of the management game. Every owned property has stash capacity. That includes illegal-focused properties and front businesses. Different properties should vary in capacity, security, laundering strength, and how useful they are as operational hubs, but every owned location can hold something of value.

For v1, the property game should focus on two major roles. Stash or trap properties act as primary illegal storage and dealer support hubs. Front businesses convert dirty money into clean money over time while still offering stash space and a safer place to move assets in an emergency.

The player should think of properties as physical nodes in a network. Product, dirty cash, clean money generation, risk, and logistics all flow through them.

### Owned Dealers
Owned dealers are part of the player's network, not just ambient NPCs. In the early management game, dealers must be supplied manually. The player brings them product from a stash and physically collects their earnings. This preserves the street-level feel while still letting the player grow beyond solo selling.

Dealers should be tied to the player's operation through assigned properties. A dealer sells out of a network that has been stocked by the player. If the stash is empty, the dealer cannot keep earning. If the player ignores collections, dirty money remains exposed in the field.

This manual dealer phase is important because it creates a real bridge between the early game and the automated late game. The player has to feel the weight of expansion before automation removes the friction.

### Runners
Runners are the first major automation layer. They do not replace the player's empire-building decisions, but they remove repetitive transport work once the player has earned that convenience.

Runners handle both product movement and cash collection. They move stock between player inventory, stash properties, and active dealers. They also collect money from the network so the player no longer has to personally do every pickup. This is what transforms a fragile operation into a scalable one.

Runners should never feel free or riskless. They cost money to hire and maintain, and their routes can fail or be intercepted. Automation increases scale, but it also creates new points of failure.

### Laundering
Dirty money and clean money are strictly separate. Street sales and illegal operations generate dirty money. Major legal purchases, formal control fees, and long-term empire growth should depend on clean money.

Laundering is not a one-click conversion. Front businesses process dirty money continuously over time based on capacity, efficiency, and risk. The player must build enough laundering throughput to support expansion. If the network generates dirty cash faster than fronts can clean it, pressure builds and exposure rises.

Laundering should feel like infrastructure. It is a core gate on growth, not a side mechanic.

### Heat and Raids
Heat is the pressure generated by illegal activity. As heat rises, police attention becomes more aggressive and operational risk increases. At small scale, heat is mostly about surviving personal encounters. At larger scale, it becomes the force that threatens the player's network.

Raids are the clearest expression of this pressure. When a property is targeted, the player receives a warning and a timer begins. The response is physical and real-time. The player must move illegal inventory, cash, or sensitive assets out of the targeted stash and into safer locations before the raid lands.

Because every owned property has stash capacity, the player can use the wider network defensively. A front business is not only for laundering. It can also become emergency shelter for exposed assets during a crisis.

### Arrest, Court, and Death Penalties
Arrest should lead to a court system that reflects both what the authorities can prove and how much the player can spend to defend themselves. Court outcomes are based on evidence, charges, prior pressure, and legal spending. Money matters, but money does not erase consequences for free.

Court penalties can include fines, seizures, operational setbacks, and other long-tail costs that punish reckless expansion without wiping out the entire empire. The goal is to create problems the player must manage, not to force total resets for every failure.

Death carries a separate penalty. When the player dies, they lose all drugs and guns they were carrying. They are also hit with a large hospital bill. That bill can be paid immediately or through daily payments until the debt is cleared. Death should hurt, but it should create recovery gameplay rather than pure frustration.

## Core Gameplay Loop
The main growth loop is straightforward. The player acquires product, makes dirty money, raises territory reputation, buys property, stocks stash locations, assigns dealers, launders money into clean cash, claims territory control, automates routes with runners, and then expands again.

Each new layer should build on the last one. The player starts by surviving. Then they start storing. Then they start controlling. Then they start automating. The loop should always feel like the same business growing larger rather than separate games stitched together.

## Pressure Loop
The pressure loop exists to stop growth from becoming passive or solved. Heat rises through illegal activity. Police attention escalates. Raids threaten stash locations and exposed cash. Gang wars challenge unstable or contested territories. Arrests create court costs and legal penalties. Death creates debt and equipment loss.

The player is always balancing expansion against exposure. Every gain in scale should create a new vulnerability somewhere else in the system. That tension is the heart of the game.

## V1 Focus
The first complete version of the game loop should stay focused. V1 should prioritize territory reputation and control, stash and front property ownership, owned dealers, runners, laundering throughput, raid warnings with stash relocation, and court or debt consequences.

Other ideas can still exist as future directions, but they should not distract from building one clear playable loop first. The goal of v1 is to make the transition from manual street dealing to hybrid network management feel complete and understandable.

## Current Project Status
The current playable prototype is still in the early game and very early transition stage. The project already supports direct street solicitation, hand-to-hand sales, dealer purchasing, territory-based pricing, territory reputation, inventory carrying, progression, in-world time, and police heat. That means the manual hustle loop exists and the game already has the right foundation for pressure and territory identity.

What does not exist yet is the ownership and operations layer that defines the target mid and end game. There is not yet a real property system, stash network, dealer assignment system, runner automation, laundering business flow, territory claim state machine, raid logistics loop, court system, or debt-driven economy layer. Those systems still need to be built on top of the current street-level foundation.

For a more detailed breakdown of what already exists and what still needs to be built, see [game-loop-status.md](C:/Users/jphil/Documents/family-business-prototype/docs/game-loop-status.md).

## Later Expansion
Once the core loop is solid, the game can expand into deeper property specialization, more business types, more detailed legal systems, more crew roles, stronger late-game faction conflict, and additional economic layers. Those are valid growth paths, but they should sit on top of the v1 loop rather than replacing or delaying it.

## Design Pillars
- Risk versus reward must stay central.
- Progression should reduce manual work without removing player agency.
- Player choice must meaningfully shape growth, safety, and territory control.
- Strong money sinks are necessary to keep scaling interesting.
- Failure should create new problems to solve, not constant full resets.

## Agent Notes
The intended game loop is simple to state. The player begins with direct street sales. The first property is the hard pivot into management. Every owned property has stash capacity. Dirty money must be laundered into clean money to support real expansion. Territory control requires 100 reputation and then either a clean-money control fee or three gang war wins. Dealers begin as manual logistics problems and become scalable through runners. Runners automate both product movement and cash collection. Raids are real-time physical emergencies. Arrests lead to court outcomes shaped by evidence and legal spending. Death removes carried drugs and guns and creates hospital debt.

Any future feature work should preserve that progression path: street hustle first, network management second, empire pressure forever.
